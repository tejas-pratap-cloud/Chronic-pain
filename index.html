What is Pain? First things first, to be clear: Pain does not equal hurt. In fact, pain is a mechanism used by your subconscious brain for protection to prevent actual damage to your tissue, because once the pain kicks in, it will stop you from doing what you were before. So, here’s how it works: The brain and nervous system decide whether to give you pain or not. This is judged from multiple factors such as messages from your nervous system, what is currently happening and what occurred when you were in this situation before. This is why sometimes even a slight injury like a papercut can cause serious pain, even though it does not feel like it should. Also think about when you kick a football, you usually do not feel any pain, which is because the brain does not feel the need to send a signal for protection. We do need pain for survival, but quite a lot of the time, your brain goes into overdrive, like an alarm: this is called persistent pain. Good news is that your brain is just trying to protect your body from actually getting hurt, but sadly it prevents you from doing the activities you know and love. So, remember: pain is 100% real and does not correlate to any damage of the body.
The Three Ns of Pain. Pain at first seems simple: your brain thinks you are hurt, you receive pain. To be honest, that is not really the case. In fact, there are 3 types of pain known as the three Ns: Nociceptive Pain, Neuropathic Pain and Nociplastic Pain. Nociceptive Pain: This is caused by actual tissue damage or inflammation. When in this type of pain, you usually receive a sharp, prickling, dull or aching sensation caused by the tissue damage. Example of these could be your standard papercut, infections or a broken bone Neuropathic Pain: The main reason of this type of pain is due to nerve damage from an injury or disease. Depending on the situation, you could experience burning, tingling or electric shock like sensations. Some examples include things like diabetic neuropathy, shingles and sciatica. Nociplastic Pain: This happens when your nervous system starts to process pain differently and has little links to tissue damage, a clear injury, inflammation or disease. The sensations of this type or pain vary widely but some examples include fibromyalgia, irritable bowel syndrome and chronic low back pain. As you can see, the three types of pain vary in many ways, but Chronic Pain (what we will talk about next) can be a result of all three!
What is Chronic Pain? Chronic Pain is when you experience Nociceptive, Neuropathic or Nociplastic pain for longer than 3 to 6 months. This can affect your physical and mental health, our social and home lives and even our ability to stay at work. No – one is totally risk free, sadly, but there are efforts going on to solve this problem. There are quite a few cool facts about this as well! Chronic pain affects 30% - 50% of us in the United Kingdom and up to 14% of people are even disabled because of this. Pain can affect anyone no matter their race, gender, age or financial situation. Some things that can increase the risk are smoking and having a hereditary condition, but keeping active can also lower the risk. Over the past 40 years, lots of discoveries have been made, but very little of this information is known by the average street person. Hence, many of our perceptions of our pain are in fact, wrong. However, the constant myths can and will be busted by new scientific research and technology.
